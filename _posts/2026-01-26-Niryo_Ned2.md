## The robotic arm 

![Ned2 side view](/mars_ned2/assets/images/niryo_ned2.png){: .align-center}

The Niryo Ned2 robotic arm is designed to mimic the six axes of the human arm which reproduced much of the flexibility and precision. As an educational robot with a 49 cm reach, 300 g payload capacity, and ±0.5 mm repeatability, the Ned2 serves as a practical platform for the project. 


## Technical Specifications

The Ned2 operates within defined performance parameters that determine its suitability for specific research and educational applications. These specifications reflect the trade-offs between educational accessibility, cost-effectiveness, and functional capability.

### Structural and Movement Characteristics

The robot's six joints enable a wide range of motion within defined angular limits. Each joint's range reflects the mechanical design and safety constraints:

The robot weighs approximately 7 kg with a compact footprint (200 × 200 mm), designed for desktop-scale research and laboratory environments.


### Processing and Connectivity

The control architecture integrates an ARM V8 processor (1.5 GHz) with 4 GB RAM running Ubuntu 20.04 LTS. This processing capability supports real-time control and sensor fusion but has inherent constraints for complex on-board machine learning or vision processing tasks. Connectivity options include Gigabit Ethernet, Wi-Fi 5, and multiple USB 3.0 ports, enabling flexible integration with external computing systems for offloaded processing or multi-robot coordination.

The robot features configurable digital and analog I/O interfaces (3 digital inputs/outputs, 2 analog inputs/outputs), allowing basic sensor integration and external device control without requiring custom hardware modifications.

## The six axes

### Axis 1: Shoulder Rotation

Reproduces horizontal shoulder rotation, allowing the arm to rotate around its vertical base axis. This movement is essential for positioning the arm across a wide working surface and provides the robot's primary azimuthal (yaw) motion. This is the fastest joint with a maximum velocity of 10.0 rad/s.

## Why six axes?

### Versatility

Six‑axis arms closely replicate human motions, allowing them to perform a wide variety of tasks, from heavy industrial work to delicate operations in microelectronics and medicine. In educational settings, the Ned2's versatility enables students to learn assembly techniques, pick-and-place operations, and precision manipulation across diverse laboratory applications.

### Operational flexibility

Being able to mimic human arm motion lets six‑axis arms adapt to different work environments and tasks with minimal mechanical or software reconfiguration. Ned2's collaborative design makes it safe for academic laboratory environments where human operators work alongside the robot.

## Accuracy, Precision, and Performance

The Ned2 achieves a repeatability specification of ±0.5 mm, meaning that when commanded to return to a previously defined position, the robot's endpoint typically deviates by no more than 0.5 millimeters. This level of precision is well-suited for educational demonstrations and research prototyping but represents a significant limitation compared to industrial collaborative robots (which typically achieve ±0.03 to ±0.1 mm repeatability).

The robot's control architecture supports both joint-space positioning (direct joint angle specification) and Cartesian-space positioning (end-effector position and orientation specification), with trajectory planning interpolation between waypoints. This flexibility enables diverse research applications while acknowledging that absolute accuracy is limited by the underlying mechanical design rather than control sophistication.


## Operational Limitations and Constraints

### Processing and Computation Constraints

The ARM V8 processor and 4 GB RAM provide sufficient capability for real-time joint control and basic sensor integration but create constraints for computationally intensive tasks. Complex machine vision algorithms or simultaneous multi-sensor fusion are better executed on external computers with the robot operating as a peripheral device. This design pattern allows flexible algorithm development and easy system reconfiguration.

## Work Envelope and Singularities

The work envelope (workspace) is the volume a robotic arm can reach, determined by the reach and limits of its axes.

### Singularities

A singularity occurs when the robot's joint configuration causes a loss of degrees of freedom, typically when multiple joints align in certain orientations. At these points, the kinematic equations break down, preventing smooth Cartesian motion and potentially causing unpredictable joint behavior. For the Ned2, singularities commonly arise when the wrist and arm joints align in a straight-line configuration, limiting the robot's ability to move freely in all directions.

To avoid issues related to singularities, common strategies include:

- **Path planning:** motion planning algorithms (such as MoveIt) compute alternative paths that avoid singular configurations before executing the motion.
- **Mechanical design:** Choose joint layouts that disperse singular points across the workspace; the Ned2's design minimises singularities in typical educational operating ranges.


### Programming and Integration Pathways

NED2 is programmable through multiple programming interfaces: 

- **Visual Programming (Blockly):** Block-based programming for introductory demonstrations and non-programmers
- **Python API (PyNiryo):** Python bindings via TCP/IP for rapid prototyping and algorithm development
- **ROS Integration (ROS Noetic):** Native ROS integration for complex multi-robot systems, sensor fusion, and research workflows
- **Simulation Environments:** RoboDK integration (included) and ROS simulation tools (Gazebo) enable offline development and validation
- **External Integration:** MATLAB, C++, and custom applications communicate via network protocols (Modbus TCP, TCP/IP)


## Safety Design and Collaborative Principles

The Ned2 is classified as a collaborative robot (cobot), designed to operate safely alongside human operators in shared workspaces. This philosophy influences several design decisions:

### Collision Detection and Response

The Ned2 integrates an **accelerometer and gyroscope in the control panel** for real-time collision monitoring. These sensors detect sudden deceleration caused by impact with obstacles or persons. Upon detection, the robot immediately stops and enters a safe state, awaiting operator intervention. This **reactive safety mechanism** complements motion planning algorithms that compute collision-free paths during autonomous operation. Unlike predictive systems, this approach prioritises inherent safety through mechanical compliance and real-time monitoring rather than complex vision-based obstacle avoidance.

### Operating Modes and Constraints

The robot operates in multiple modes with different safety characteristics:
- **Manual Mode:** Direct human control with real-time feedback
- **FreeMotion Mode:** Gravity-compensated manual positioning for intuitive teaching
- **Calibration Mode:** Safe initialization and diagnostic operations
- **Program Mode:** Autonomous operation with enforced workspace and speed limits

These modes provide graduated autonomy, allowing operators to choose safety levels appropriate to the task and environment.

### Safety Philosophy

Unlike traditional industrial robots (which require safety cages and restricted access), the Ned2's design philosophy emphasises inherent safety through mechanical compliance, power limitations, and real-time monitoring. This approach makes the robot more practical our educational and research environments where frequent human interaction and task reconfiguration are necessary.
